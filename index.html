Hello!

This is a pre-alpha prototype tool designed to help you articulate what you care about - in general, and then in relation to this new product you’re working on. It comes from the VIRT-EU project work and is just one in a series of different tools to try bring ethical reflection into the process of designing connected things.

So you submitted a product sketch for the challenge of “Designing for Difference in the Connected Home.”

“Design”, “connected”, “smart” shared homes. Let’s dig a little deeper. What does it mean to add smartness, connectivity and intelligence to the devices we design? And what does it mean when we add those devices into communal living spaces?

At this moment when headlines around tech gone wrong flood our news daily, let’s reflect. What is a good product, to you? Does good only mean functions well? Or does a good product align with your idea of what goodness even means, in the first place?

Let’s find some North stars, as understood through your values. Hopefully the work together will be useful as you keep working on your idea for the Design Challenge.

Saying What You Care About

This round is a solo activity. Keeping in mind what you have read, and what you learned or felt from that discussion, write down your values. This is for you, as a person, in life in general.

Here are some helper prompts:

A good life for me unfolds when I choose to be ____.

The world would be better if everyone ____.

I [read about] [heard about] ___ and it made me care about ___.

Each individual takes a post it of the same color.
Write a big word + example of when you saw this happening or not

Cluster Negotiation


If definitions of same words are different, can you agree upon one of the definitions?

If the two definitions are strongly held “positions”, you may need to negotiate the difference. Discuss your definitions and try to re-write together.

Some pointers for discussion:
When you respond to the other, try saying things like “It sounds like you are concerned about ___”
Try to talk in a way that will encourage people to say “Yes, that’s right,” which shows they see that you understand them.

Remember this is your teammate and try to show that you recognize and respect each others’ opinions, distinct backgrounds, mutual admiration and goal of building something together
